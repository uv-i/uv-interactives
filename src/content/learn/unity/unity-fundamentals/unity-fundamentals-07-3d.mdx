The 3D module assembles a real game slice: a controllable character, a camera that
follows properly, and an enemy with a brain. Three systems, three industry-standard
tools.

## CharacterController — the deliberate physics cheat

Open **`Module07_3D/Scenes/01_ThirdPerson`**. `ThirdPersonController.cs` does NOT
use a Rigidbody. A **CharacterController** is a capsule that moves *exactly* where
told, auto-handles steps and slopes, and exposes `isGrounded` for free. The trade:
you simulate gravity yourself —

```csharp
_velocity.y += gravity * Time.deltaTime;
_controller.Move(_velocity * Time.deltaTime);
```

The design rule: **Rigidbody = realistic object. CharacterController = character who
politely ignores physics for playability.** Players don't want realistic movement;
they want *responsive* movement.

**Camera-relative movement** is the second half: pressing W must mean "toward where
the camera looks", not world-north. The angle math adds the camera's Y rotation to
the input angle, smoothed with `SmoothDampAngle`. Try commenting the camera term out
— movement instantly feels broken. That's the lesson.

The camera itself: **Cinemachine**. Create → Cinemachine Camera, set Follow and
Look At to the player — done. No camera code, no jitter, no wall-clipping. (Unity 6
uses Cinemachine 3.x: the component is `CinemachineCamera`, not the older
`CinemachineVirtualCamera` you'll see in old tutorials.)

## NavMesh — giving the world a map

Open **`02_NavMeshAI`**. Pathfinding in three steps:

1. Mark floors/obstacles **Navigation Static**.
2. **Window → AI → Navigation → Bake** — the blue overlay that appears IS the map
   of everywhere an agent can walk.
3. Put a **NavMeshAgent** on the enemy and call `agent.SetDestination(target)` —
   Unity plans the path, walks it, and avoids other agents.

`EnemyAI3D.cs` runs a state machine on top — the enum + switch pattern:
**Patrol** (walk waypoints) → **Chase** (player within the yellow Gizmo sphere) →
**Attack** (player within the red one). Distance checks drive the transitions. This
exact structure — an enum, a switch, transition conditions — is how most game AI
below AAA-scale actually ships.

Waypoints are just empty GameObjects dragged into the agent's array — place 3–4
around the level and watch it loop.

## Melee combat — the hitbox pattern

Open **`03_Combat`**. `MeleeAttack.cs` implements the hitbox used in every action
game: an empty child object at the hand with a **trigger SphereCollider that stays
disabled**. Attacking enables it for 0.2 seconds (a coroutine — Module 3 again),
`OnTriggerEnter` deals damage through the same `HealthSystem` from Module 3, then it
disables. Why not always-on? An enabled hitbox damages everything near you every
frame — the enable-window IS the attack timing.

## Try it

Add a fourth state: **Flee** — when the enemy's own health drops below 30%, it runs
*away* from the player (SetDestination in the opposite direction). One enum entry,
one case, one condition. If that felt easy, the state machine did its job.

## Interview corner

**CharacterController vs Rigidbody for players?** Controller: precise, steps/slopes
free, immune to forces. Rigidbody: physical realism, pushable. Players usually want
the controller.

**What does baking a NavMesh do?** Pre-computes walkable polygons from static
geometry into an asset agents query at runtime; re-bake when static geometry changes.

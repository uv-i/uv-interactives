Unity's 2D side is a parallel universe: its own physics (`Rigidbody2D`, `Collider2D`
— the 3D and 2D systems never interact), its own level editor, and its own dark art:
*game feel*. This module builds a platformer that feels right, and teaches you why.

## Tilemaps — painting levels

Open **`Module06_2D/Scenes/01_Tilemap`**. The 2D level pipeline, start to finish:

1. Import a sprite sheet (free: kenney.nl's Platformer Pack) → Inspector →
   **Sprite Mode: Multiple** (the step everyone forgets).
2. **Sprite Editor → Slice → Grid By Cell Size** — one image becomes many sprites.
3. Drag them into a **Tile Palette** (**Window → 2D → Tile Palette**), then paint
   your level onto a Tilemap like Photoshop with collision.

Then the collider trick that separates working games from janky ones: add **Tilemap
Collider 2D**, then **Composite Collider 2D** (its Rigidbody2D set to *Static*), and
tick **Used By Composite** on the tilemap collider. Without it, every tile has its own
tiny collider and characters *catch on the seams* between tiles. Composite merges them
into one smooth outline.

## The controller — three invisible features

Open **`02_PlayerController`** and read `PlayerController2D.cs`. Three mechanisms,
none of which players ever see — and all of which they *feel*:

**Coyote time.** After walking off a ledge you get a 0.15s window where jump still
works — named for Wile E. Coyote hovering past the cliff edge. Human reaction time
means players press jump a few frames "too late" constantly; without this grace
window the game feels unfairly slippery. A timer resets while grounded and counts
down in the air; jump is allowed while it's positive.

**Jump buffering.** Press jump slightly *before* landing and a 0.1s buffer remembers
it, executing the jump the frame you touch down. Without it, early presses vanish
and the controls feel unresponsive.

**Variable jump height.** Release the jump key early → upward velocity halves →
short hop. Hold → full jump. One `if` statement, in Super Mario Bros since 1985.

Ground detection here uses `Physics2D.OverlapCircle` — a circle at the feet checking
a ground layer. More forgiving than a single ray on uneven tile terrain.

## The full scene

Open **`03_FullScene`**: `EnemyPatrol2D` (walks a range, flips at the edges — watch
its Gizmo line in Scene view), `Collectible2D` (bobs on a sine wave —
`Mathf.Sin(Time.time * speed) * height`, a pattern you'll reuse forever), and
`DeathZone2D` (a wide trigger under the level that reloads the scene — every
platformer's out-of-bounds handler, in its simplest form).

## Try it

Set coyote time to 0 in the Inspector and play your own level for two minutes. Then
set it to 0.15 and play again. You now understand game feel at a level most players
never will — and you'll never ship a platformer without it.

## Interview corner

**What is coyote time and why implement it?** A 100–200ms post-ledge jump grace
window compensating for human reaction time; without it edges feel slippery and
unfair.

**Why Composite Collider 2D on tilemaps?** Per-tile colliders create micro-seams
that catch characters; Composite merges them into one smooth polygon.
